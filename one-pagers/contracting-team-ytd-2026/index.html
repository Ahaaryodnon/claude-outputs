The three things to take away

Volume is up, estimation is sharper, cycle time has a tail problem

1

Volume is real, not optical

Aaron closed 72 items / 120 points across 10 sprints — first by points on the team, ahead of Smita (105) and Praise (102). Average sprint contribution sits at 12 points, well clear of the team mean of ~5. This isn't a one-sprint spike; the work is distributed across the year.

2

Estimation discipline moved in the right direction

Unestimated rate on Aaron's items is 15.3% versus a team average of 28.1%. That's a meaningful gap — items go into sprint with story points attached, which makes capacity planning honest. Worth defending: the temptation to drop estimation on small spikes is real, but the team-wide rate shows the cost of it.

3

Cycle-time mean is a tail problem, not a throughput problem

Average cycle time is 20.4 days against a team average of 12.2. But the median is 7.7 days — most items close quickly. The mean is being dragged by a handful of long-running items, likely Features that have been activated but parked. The fix is operational: triage the open tail, close or split anything older than four weeks. Don't slow down day-to-day work to chase the average.

Context for the numbers

What the dashboard tells us about the team, not just Aaron

Across the team, completed-work distribution looks healthy at the top — six people sit between 70 and 105 points, which is a wider strong shoulder than this team had at the same point in 2025. The unestimated rate at 28.1% team-wide is the single biggest hygiene issue: it's mostly concentrated in a small number of contributors and a small set of work types (Tasks and Incidents). A short cleanup pass would move this number a long way.

Cycle time tells a similar story at team level: median 7.7d across all completed items is fine, mean 12.2d shows a long tail. Spotting and triaging that tail every fortnight would do more for predictability than any process change.
