The reflection at a glance — Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

1

Description

Twelve weekly rounds, two-week lead time, three cost types. A round-3 demand spike (240 vs 180 in stock) caused our first stockout; the panic double-order arrived in round 6 as demand normalised, leaving 300+ units on the shelf. From round 8 we switched to a three-round moving average, an explicit reorder point, and a 40-unit safety buffer.

2

Feelings

Quiet confidence going in, embarrassment after arguing our stock was "comfortable" the round before the stockout, frustration watching holding costs pay weekly for one moment of panic — and, by the end, earned confidence: good inventory management feels boring.

3

Evaluation

Good: the round-8 recovery, explicit safety stock, and clear role division in five-minute rounds. Bad: seven reactive rounds, forecasting from a single week, and ignoring the lead time — ordering for a world that had already changed by the time stock arrived.

4

Analysis

Textbook bullwhip: demand-signal processing (Lee et al., 1997) and supply-line under-weighting (Croson & Donohue, 2006) explain the round-4 double-order placed while stock was already in transit. The recovery worked because it matched theory — newsvendor cost-ratio logic justified the 40-unit buffer, and consistent batches approximated EOQ.

5

Conclusion

A mediocre policy applied consistently beats good intuition applied reactively. Costs were highest not when demand was hardest to predict, but when every decision was made from scratch under pressure. One vivid failure outweighed seven weeks of stable data in my judgement.

6

Action plan

Build a simple model before committing to any quantitative decision; write down what's in the pipeline to stop under-weighting the supply line; propose an agreed decision rule in the first session of the next group project; practise stating data-based dissent clearly, once, before the group commits.

Three lessons that transfer

Respect the supply line

Every early mistake traced back to ignoring stock already ordered but not yet arrived. Orders must cover the protection interval — lead time plus review period — not the current week.

Commit to a decision rule

The improvement came less from the sophistication of the model than from the discipline of having one. Adjust the parameters; don't override the policy.

Beware the vivid event

I over-correct after visible failures. One dramatic stockout drove more decisions than seven weeks of stable demand data — a bias a written forecast keeps in check.
